Section 7.5 Comparison Tests

Motivating Questions

  • How can we use series which are known to converge or diverge to test for convergence of more complicated series?

  • What is the limit comparison test and how can it be applied to determine convergence or divergence of a given series?

In Section 7.4, we encountered more infinite series and began discussing how to test for convergence or divergence using the divergence and integral tests. But what can we do when presented with series that are similar to simpler series that we know converge or diverge, such as \(p\)-series, but that we are unable to apply a different test to? In this section, we will consider two tests which involve comparing the convergence or divergence of two series -- the direct comparison test and the limit comparison test.

Preview Activity 7.5.1.

  1. Consider the series

    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{k-2}{k^4+1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Since the convergence or divergence of a series only depends on the behavior of the series for large values of \(k\text{,}\) we might examine the terms of this series more closely as \(k\) gets large. By computing the value of \(\frac{k-2}{k^4+1}\) for \(k=100\) and \(k=1000\text{,}\) explain why the terms \(\frac{k-2}{k^4+1}\) are essentially \(\frac{k}{k^4}\) when \(k\) is large.

  2. This gives us the sense that the series \(\sum \frac{k-2}{k^4+1}\) should behave the same way that \(\sum \frac{k}{k^4} = \sum \frac{1}{k^3}\) behaves. Does \(\sum \frac{1}{k^3}\) converge or diverge? Why?

  3. Explain why for all \(k \geq 2\text{,}\) \(0 \leq \frac{k-2}{k^4+1} \leq \frac{k}{k^4} = \frac{1}{k^3}\text{.}\)

  4. Suppose that \(S_n = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{k-2}{k^4+1}\) and \(T_n = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k^3}\) are partial sums for the series \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{k-2}{k^2+1}\) and \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^3}\) respectively. Explain why we have \(S_n \leq T_n\) for all values of \(n \geq 1\text{.}\)

  5. Does the sequence \(\{ T_n \}\) converge or diverge? Why?

  6. We now so that the series \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{k-2}{k^4+1}\) is convergent by showing the sequence of partial sums \(\{S_n\}\) converge. Suppose that \(T = \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} T_n\text{.}\) Then, since each term in the partial sum \(T_n\) is positive, we have that \(\{ T_n \}\) is an increasing sequence and \(T_n \leq T\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Show that the sequence \(\{S_n\}\) is monotone and bounded and hence also converges.

Subsection 7.5.1 The Direct Comparison Test

The Integral Test in Section 7.4 allows us to determine the convergence of an entire family of series: the \(p\)-series. However, we have seen that it is often difficult to integrate functions, so the Integral Test is not one that we can use all of the time. In fact, even for a relatively simple series such as \(\sum \frac{k^2+1}{k^4+2k+2}\text{,}\) the Integral Test is not an option. In what follows we develop a test that applies to series of rational functions by comparing their behavior to that of other series whose convergence behavior we know.

Example 7.5.1.

Determine whether the series

\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2+1} \end{equation*}

converges or diverges.

Solution.

This series looks similar to the convergent series \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2}\text{.}\) Since the terms in each of these series are positive, the sequence of partial sums for each series is monotone increasing. Also, since

\begin{equation*} 0 < \frac{1}{k^2+1} < \frac{1}{k^2} \end{equation*}

for all positive integers \(k\text{,}\) the \(n\)th partial sum \(S_n\) of \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2+1}\) satisfies

\begin{equation*} S_n = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k^2+1} < \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k^2} < \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Values of the first \(8\) partial sums of these two series are found in Table 7.5.2.

Values from Table 7.5.2 are plotted in Figure 7.5.3.

Since the "larger" series, \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2}\text{,}\) converges, the sequence \(\{ S_n\}\) of partial sums is bounded above. In fact, since each term in the series is positive, \(\{ S_n \}\) is a monotone increasing sequence that is bounded above. As we saw in what is often referred to as the Monotone Convergence Theorem,  \(\{S_n \}\) converges. Thus, since the partial sums converge, the series \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2+1}\) converges.

The Direct Comparison Test.

Suppose that \(0 \leq a_k \leq b_k\) for all \(k\) beyond a certain value.

  • If \(\sum b_k\) converges, then \(\sum a_k\) converges.

  • If \(\sum a_k\) diverges, then \(\sum b_k\) diverges.

The idea behind the direct comparison test is very intuitive. If a series \(\sum a_k\) has terms that are all (beyond a certain \(k\)) smaller than the corresponding terms in a series \(\sum b_k\) that we know converges, then the unknown series \(\sum a_k\) must also converge, because it is "smaller" than something finite. Thus, we can use the first bullet point above to prove that a series (\(\sum a_k\)) converges.

On the other hand, if a series \(\sum b_k\) has terms that are all (beyond a certain \(k\)) larger than the corresponding terms in a series \(\sum a_k\) that we know diverges, then the series \(\sum b_k\) must also diverge, because it is "larger" than something infinite. Thus, we can use the second bullet point to prove that a series (\(\sum b_n\)) diverges.

In Preview Activity 7.5.1, we saw that the series \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{k-2}{k^4+1}\) converges since \(0 \leq \frac{k-2}{k^4+1} \leq \frac{1}{k^3}\) for \(k \geq 2\) and \(\sum \frac{1}{k^3}\) converges by virtue of being a \(p\)-series with \(p=3\text{.}\)

Example 7.5.4.

Determine whether

\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{3k+2}{4k^2-3} \end{equation*}

converges or diverges.

Solution.

We first get a feel for what we expect the series to do. For very large \(k\text{,}\) \(\frac{3k+2}{4k^2-3}\) behaves like \(\frac{3k}{4k^2} = \frac{3}{4k} = \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{k}\text{.}\) Since \(\sum \frac{1}{k}\) diverges, then \(\sum \frac{3}{4k}\) also diverges, so we expect \(\sum \frac{3k+2}{4k^2 -3}\) to diverge as well.

Our goal is to build the inequality

\begin{equation*} \frac{3k+2}{4k^2-3} \geq \cdots \geq 0 \end{equation*}

where the middle is filled in with a series that we know diverges. To make a fraction smaller, we should decrease the numerator and increase the denominator. So we can build the inequality

\begin{equation*} \frac{3k+2}{4k^2-3} \geq \frac{3k}{4k^2-3} \geq \frac{3k}{4k^2} \geq \frac{3}{4k} \geq 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now since \(\sum \frac{3}{4n}\) diverges, then \(\sum \frac{3k+2}{4k^2-3}\) also diverges by the direct comparison test.

In order to use the direct comparison test, we have to be able to set up the inequalities requried by the test, which can sometimes be difficult.

Example 7.5.5.

Consider the series \(\sum \frac{k+1}{k^3+2}\text{.}\)

  1. For large \(k\text{,}\) what \(p\)-series does \(\sum \frac{k+1}{k^3+2}\) behave like? Do you expect \(\sum \frac{k+1}{k^3+2}\) to converge or diverge?

  2. Set up an inequality to apply the direct comparison test to prove that the series converges or diverges. What makes this challenging?

Solution.

  1. For large \(k\text{,}\) \(\frac{k+1}{k^3+2}\) behaves like \(\frac{k}{k^3} = \frac{1}{k^2}\text{.}\) Since \(\sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) is a \(p\)-series with \(p=2\text{,}\) then \(\sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) converges, so we expect \(\frac{k+1}{k^3+2}\) to converge as well.

  2. We need to find a series whose \(k\)th term is larger than \(\frac{k+1}{k^3+2}\text{.}\) We can begin with

    \begin{equation*} 0 \leq \frac{k+1}{k^3+2} \leq \frac{k+1}{k^3} \end{equation*}

    for all \(k \geq 1\text{.}\) However, \(\frac{k}{k^3}\) is smaller than \(\frac{k+1}{k^3}\text{,}\) not larger. Thus, we need to make more of an effort to find a useful inequality. For example,

    \begin{equation*} 0 \leq \frac{k+1}{k^3+2} \leq \frac{k+1}{k^3} \leq \frac{k+k}{k^3} = \frac{2k}{k^3}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    This works because in the numerator, \(k+1 \leq k + k\) for \(k \geq 1\text{.}\) Then \(\frac{2k}{k^3} =2 \left( \frac{1}{k^2} \right)\text{,}\) which yields a convergent \(p\)-series. By the direct comparison test, \(\sum \frac{k+1}{k^3 + 2}\) also converges.

Subsection 7.5.2 The Limit Comparison Test

The limit comparison test is another test that can be used to determine series convergence and works in many of the same situations that the direct comparison test is used. However, the limit comparison test does not require any inequalities between the series you are comparing, so in some cases it can be easier to use than the direct comparison test.

Activity 7.5.3.

Once again, consider the series \(\sum \frac{k+1}{k^3+2}\text{.}\)

  1. Recall that for large \(k\text{,}\) \(\frac{k+1}{k^3+2}\) looks like \(\frac{k}{k^3}\text{.}\) Let's formalize this observation a bit more. Let \(a_k = \frac{k+1}{k^3+2}\) and \(b_k = \frac{k}{k^3}\text{.}\) Calculate

    \begin{equation*} \lim_{k \to \infty} \frac{a_k}{b_k}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    What does the value of the limit tell you about \(a_k\) and \(b_k\) for large values of \(k\text{?}\)

  2. What do you think that tells us about the convergence or divergence of the series \(\sum \frac{k+1}{k^3+2}\text{,}\) based on the convergence of the series \(\sum \frac{k}{k^3}\text{?}\) Explain.

Activity 7.5.3 illustrates how we can compare one series with positive terms to another whose convergence status we know. Suppose we have two series \(\sum a_k\) and \(\sum b_k\) with positive terms and we know the convergence status of the series \(\sum a_k\text{.}\) Recall that the convergence or divergence of a series depends only on the terms of the series for large values of \(k\text{,}\) so if we know that \(a_k\) and \(b_k\) are proportional for large \(k\text{,}\) then the two series \(\sum a_k\) and \(\sum b_k\) should behave the same way. In other words, if there is a positive finite constant \(c\) such that

\begin{equation*} \lim_{k \to \infty} \frac{b_k}{a_k} = c\text{,} \end{equation*}

then \(b_k \approx ca_k\) for large values of \(k\text{.}\) So

\begin{equation*} \sum b_k \approx \sum ca_k = c \sum a_k\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since multiplying by a nonzero constant does not affect the convergence or divergence of a series, it follows that the series \(\sum a_k\) and \(\sum b_k\) either both converge or both diverge. The formal statement of this fact is called the limit comparison test.

The Limit Comparison Test.

Let \(\sum a_k\) and \(\sum b_k\) be series with positive terms. If

\begin{equation*} \lim_{k \to \infty} \frac{b_k}{a_k} = c \end{equation*}

for some positive (finite) constant \(c\text{,}\) then \(\sum a_k\) and \(\sum b_k\) either both converge or both diverge.

The limit comparison test shows that if we have a series \(\sum \frac{p(k)}{q(k)}\) of rational functions where \(p(k)\) is a polynomial of degree \(m\) and \(q(k)\) a polynomial of degree \(l\text{,}\) then the series \(\sum \frac{p(k)}{q(k)}\) will behave like the series \(\sum \frac{k^m}{k^l}\text{.}\) So this test allows us to determine the convergence or divergence of series whose terms are rational functions.

Subsection 7.5.3 Summary

  • Even when possible, applying the integral test can be difficult due to integration involved. We can in many cases instead use the direct comparison test to compare the convergence of a challenging series with a much simpler \(p\)-series. In particular, if \(0 \leq a_k \leq b_k\) for all \(k\text{,}\) then

    1. If \(\sum b_k\) converges, then \(\sum a_k\) also converges.

    2. If \(\sum a_k\) diverges, then \(\sum b_k\) also diverges.

  • The limit comparison test applies in many of the same situations as the comparison test, but does not require showing any inequality relationship between the two series. If \(\lim_{k \rightarrow \infty} \frac{b_k}{a_k} = c\) for some positive (finite) constant \(c\text{,}\) then \(\sum a_k\) and \(\sum b_k\) either both converge or both diverge.
