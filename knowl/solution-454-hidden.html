Since \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^{n-2}\) is a (convergent) geometric series, we focus our attention on the series

\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n(n+1)}\text{.} \end{equation*}

To that end, we investigate the sequence of partial sums \(\left\{ S_k \right\}\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} S_1 = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} = \frac{1}{2}\\ S_2 = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{1\cdot 2} + \frac{1}{2\cdot 3} = \frac{2}{3}\\ S_3 = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} + \frac{1}{2 \cdot 3} + \frac{1}{3\cdot 4} = \frac{3}{4}\\ S_4 = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} + \frac{1}{2 \cdot 3} + \frac{1}{3\cdot 4} + \frac{1}{4\cdot 5}= \frac{4}{5}\\ S_5 = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} + \frac{1}{2 \cdot 3} + \frac{1}{3\cdot 4} + \frac{1}{4\cdot 5} + \frac{1}{5\cdot 6} = \frac{5}{6}. \end{align*}

From this pattern, we can see that the \(k\)th partial sum is given by the explicit formula

\begin{equation*} S_k = \frac{k}{k+1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(\frac{k}{k+1} \longrightarrow 1\text{,}\) we conclude that the sequence of partial sums converges, and therefore the infinite series converges to \(1\text{.}\) That is,

\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n(n+1)} = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since both series converge, we can apply the properties of convergent series to evaluate

\begin{align*} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left[ \frac{1}{n(n+1)} + \left( \frac{1}{2}\right)^{n-2} \right] = \mathstrut \amp \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n(n+1)} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( \frac{1}{2}\right)^{n-2} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n(n+1)} + \left( \frac{1}{2}\right)^{-1} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( \frac{1}{2}\right)^{n-1}\\ = \mathstrut \amp 1 + \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^{-1} (2)\\ = \mathstrut \amp 5. \end{align*}
