1. Here \(a_k = \frac{10^k}{k!}\text{.}\) So,

    \begin{equation*} \frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k} = \frac{10^{k+1}}{(k+1)!} \cdot \frac{k!}{10^k} = \frac{10}{k+1} \rightarrow 0 \end{equation*}

    as \(k \rightarrow \infty\text{.}\) By the ratio test, the series converges and behaves like a geometric series with common ratio \(r=0\text{.}\)

  2. Here \(a_k = \frac{k^k}{k!}\text{.}\) So,

    \begin{equation*} \frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k} = \frac{(k+1)^{k+1}}{(k+1)!} \cdot \frac{k!}{k^k} = \frac{(k+1)^k}{k^k} = \left(1+\frac{1}{k} \right)^k \rightarrow e \end{equation*}

    as \(k \rightarrow \infty\text{.}\) By the ratio test, the series converges and behaves like a geometric series with common ratio \(r=e\text{.}\)

  3. Here \(a_k = \frac{(-1)^k(k!)^2}{(2k)!}\text{.}\) So,

    \begin{equation*} \frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k} = \frac{(-1)^{k+1}[(k+1)!]^2}{[2(k+1)]!} \cdot \frac{(2k)!}{(-1)^k(k!)^2} = \frac{(-1)(k+1)^2}{(2k+2)(2k+1)} = -\frac{k^2+2k+1}{4k^2+6k+2} \rightarrow -\frac{1}{4} \end{equation*}

    as \(k \rightarrow \infty\text{.}\) By the ratio test, the series converges and behaves like a geometric series with common ratio \(r=-\frac{1}{4}\text{.}\)
