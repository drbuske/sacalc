We write \(f(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+2t}} = (1+2t)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\text{.}\) Note that the (well-known) Taylor series about \(x=0\) for the function \((1+x)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\) is given by

\begin{equation*} (1+x)^{-\frac{1}{2}} = 1 + \left( -\frac{1}{2} \right)x + \frac{ \left( -\frac{1}{2} \right) \left( -\frac{3}{2} \right)}{2!} x^2 + \frac{ \left( -\frac{1}{2} \right) \left( -\frac{3}{2} \right) \left( -\frac{5}{2} \right)}{3!} x^3+\cdots \end{equation*}

This series converges for \(x\) in the interval \((-1,1)\text{.}\) Substituting \(2t\) for \(x\) yields

\begin{equation*} (1+2t)^{-\frac{1}{2}} = 1 + \left( -\frac{1}{2} \right)(2t) + \frac{ \left( -\frac{1}{2} \right) \left( -\frac{3}{2} \right)}{2!} (2t)^2 + \frac{ \left( -\frac{1}{2} \right) \left( -\frac{3}{2} \right) \left( -\frac{5}{2} \right)}{3!} (2t)^3+\cdots \end{equation*}

which simplifies to

\begin{equation*} (1+2t)^{-\frac{1}{2}} = 1 -t + \frac{ 3 \cdot 1}{2!} t^2 - \frac{ 5 \cdot 3 \cdot 1}{3!} t^3+ \frac{ 7 \cdot 5 \cdot 3 \cdot 1}{4!} t^4 + \cdots \end{equation*}

For the series to converge, we need \(2t\) to fall in the interval \((-1,1)\text{,}\) which means the interval of convergence is \((-1/2,1/2)\text{.}\)
