1. \(f(0)=a_0\text{,}\) \(f'(0)=a_1\text{,}\) \(f''(0)= 2!a_2\text{,}\) \(f'''(0)=3!a_3\text{,}\) and \(f^{(4)}{0)=4!a_4\)

  2. \begin{align*} f'(x) \amp = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} ka_kx^{k-1} \\ f''(x) \amp = \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} k(k-1)a_kx^{k-2} \\ f'''(x) \amp = \sum_{k=3}^{\infty} k(k-1)(k-2)a_kx^{k-3} \\ \vdots \amp = \vdots \\ f^{(n)}(x) \amp = \sum_{k=n}^{\infty} k(k-1)(k-2) \cdots (k-n+1)a_kx^{k-n} \\ \vdots \amp = \vdots \end{align*}

    So,

    \begin{align*} f(0) \amp = a_0 \\ f'(0) \amp = a_1 \\ f''(0) \amp = 2!a_2 \\ f'''(0) \amp = 3!a_3\\ \vdots \amp = \vdots \\ f^{(n)}(0) \amp = n!a_n \\ \vdots \amp = \vdots \end{align*}

  3. From (b), we have

    \begin{equation*} a_k = \frac{f^{(k)}(0)}{k!} \end{equation*}

    for each \(k \geq 0\text{.}\) But these are just the coefficients of the Taylor series expansion of \(f\text{,}\) which leads us to the given statement.
