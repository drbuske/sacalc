We know that to compute the area of a 2D region, we can slice it into a large number of thin rectangles and then sum (i.e. integrate) the area of these rectangles. The same principle gave us a method for computing the volume of a 3D solid using washers or shells: slice the solid into thin cross-sections, and then sum (i.e. integrate) the volume of the cross-sections. When computing area, this perspective gave us the formula

\begin{equation*} {\text{ Area}} = \int_a^b f(x) \ dx \end{equation*}

where \(f(x) \ dx\) represents the area of a thin rectangle at \(x\) and \(a\) and \(b\) are the endpoints of the interval. The analogous formula for volume is

\begin{equation*} {\text{Volume}} = \int_a^b A(x) \ dx \end{equation*}

where \(A(x) \ dx\) is the volume of a thin cross-section at \(x\) and \(a\) and \(b\) are the endpoints of the interval over which we are slicing.
