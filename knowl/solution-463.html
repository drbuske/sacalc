Solution 7.5.1.1.

This series looks similar to the convergent series \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2}\text{.}\) Since the terms in each of these series are positive, the sequence of partial sums for each series is monotone increasing. Also, since

\begin{equation*} 0 < \frac{1}{k^2+1} < \frac{1}{k^2} \end{equation*}

for all positive integers \(k\text{,}\) the \(n\)th partial sum \(S_n\) of \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2+1}\) satisfies

\begin{equation*} S_n = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k^2+1} < \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k^2} < \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since the "larger" series, \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2}\text{,}\) converges, the sequence \(\{ S_n\}\) of partial sums is bounded above. In fact, since each term in the series is positive, \(\{ S_n \}\) is a monotone increasing sequence that is bounded above. As we saw in what is often referred to as the Monotone Convergence Theorem,  \(\{S_n \}\) converges. Thus, since the partial sums converge, the series \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2+1}\) converges.
