The rectangular coordinate system can be thought of as a means to provide directions to a particular location in the plane. From a "home" position (the origin), if we walked \(1\) unit right and \(\sqrt{3}\) units down to reach a location \(P\text{,}\) we would denote such directions by the ordered pair \((1,-\sqrt{3})\) (see FigureÂ 9.3.1). But there are other ways we could describe how to reach point \(P\) from the origin. If we are standing at the origin facing to the right (in the direction of the positive \(x\)-axis), we could reach \(P\) by turning our body \(\frac{\pi}{3}\) radians clockwise and walking \(2\) units forward. Such directions could be represented by the ordered pair \(\left( 2,-\frac{\pi}{3}\right)\text{.}\) We use a negative sign for the angle of rotation since counterclockwise is normally treated positively in mathematics. These ordered pairs, or directions, are the polar coordinates we study in this section.
