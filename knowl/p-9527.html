Substituting \(x^2\) for \(x\) in the Taylor series expansion for \(\sin(x)\) gives us

\begin{equation*} \sin(x^2) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^k \frac{x^{2(2k+1)}}{(2k+1)!}\text{,} \end{equation*}

as the Taylor series for \(\sin(x^2)\) centered at \(x=0\text{.}\) We can the integrate this Taylor series term by term on its interval of convergence to find

\begin{align*} \int \sin(x^2) \ dx \amp = \int \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^k \frac{x^{2(2k+1)}}{(2k+1)!} \ dx\\ \amp = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \int (-1)^k \frac{x^{2(2k+1)}}{(2k+1)!} \ dx\\ \amp = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \int (-1)^k \frac{x^{4k+2)}}{(2k+1)!} \ dx\\ \amp = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^k \frac{x^{4k+3)}}{(2k+1)!(4k+3)} + C \end{align*}
