Section 9.3 Polar Coordinates

Motivating Questions

  • What are the polar coordinates of a point in two-space?

  • How do we convert between polar coordinates and rectangular coordinates?

  • How do we sketch polar curves from given equations?

The rectangular coordinate system can be thought of as a means to provide directions to a particular location in the plane. From a "home" position (the origin), if we walked \(1\) unit right and \(\sqrt{3}\) units down to reach a location \(P\text{,}\) we would denote such directions by the ordered pair \((1,-\sqrt{3})\) (see Figure 9.3.1). But there are other ways we could describe how to reach point \(P\) from the origin. If we are standing at the origin facing to the right (in the direction of the positive \(x\)-axis), we could reach \(P\) by turning our body \(\frac{\pi}{3}\) radians clockwise and walking \(2\) units forward. Such directions could be represented by the ordered pair \(\left( 2,-\frac{\pi}{3}\right)\text{.}\) We use a negative sign for the angle of rotation since counterclockwise is normally treated positively in mathematics. These ordered pairs, or directions, are the polar coordinates we study in this section.

Preview Activity 9.3.1.

Subsection 9.3.1 Polar Coordinates

The rectangular coordinate system is best suited for graphs and regions that are naturally considered over a rectangular grid. The polar coordinate system is an alternative that offers good options for functions and domains that have more circular characteristics. A point \(P\) in rectangular coordinates that is described by an ordered pair \((x,y)\text{,}\) where \(x\) is the displacement from \(P\) to the \(y\)-axis and \(y\) is the displacement from \(P\) to the \(x\)-axis, as seen in Preview Activity 9.3.1, can also be described with polar coordinates \((r,\theta)\text{,}\) where \(r\) is the distance from \(P\) to the origin and \(\theta\) is the angle formed by the line segment \(\overline{OP}\) and the positive \(x\)-axis, as shown at left in Figure 9.3.2.

Trigonometry and the Pythagorean Theorem allow for straightforward conversion from rectangular to polar, and vice versa.

Converting between rectangular and polar coordinates.

  • Converting from rectangular to polar.

    If we are given the rectangular coordinates \((x,y)\) of a point \(P\text{,}\) then the polar coordinates \((r,\theta)\) of \(P\) satisfy

    \begin{equation} r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2} \ \ \ \ \ \text{ and } \ \ \ \ \ \tan(\theta) = \frac{y}{x}, \text{ assuming } x \neq 0.\tag{9.6} \end{equation}
  • Converting from polar to rectangular.

    If we are given the polar coordinates \((r,\theta)\) of a point \(P\text{,}\) then the rectangular coordinates \((x,y)\) of \(P\) satisfy

    \begin{equation} x = r\cos(\theta) \ \ \ \ \ \text{ and } \ \ \ \ \ y = r\sin(\theta).\tag{9.7} \end{equation}

Note: The angle \(\theta\) in the polar coordinates of a point is not unique. We could replace \(\theta\) with \(\theta + 2 \pi\) and still be at the same terminal point. In addition, the sign of \(\tan(\theta)\) does not uniquely determine the quadrant in which \(\theta\) lies, so we have to determine the value of \(\theta\) from the location of the point. In other words, more care has to be paid when using polar coordinates than rectangular coordinates.

Example 9.3.3. Converting from rectangular to polar coordinates.

Convert each of the following points from rectangular to polar coordinates.

  1. \(\displaystyle (1,1)\)

  2. \(\displaystyle (-3,4)\)

  3. \(\displaystyle (0,3)\)

  4. \(\displaystyle (5\sqrt{3},-5)\)

Solution.

  1. Using equations (9.6), with \(x=1\) and \(y=1\) gives \(r^2=1^2+1^2=2\) or \(r=\sqrt{2}\) and \(\tan\theta = \frac{1}{1} = 1\) or \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{4}\text{.}\) The point \((1,1)\) can be represented as \(\left( \sqrt{2}, \frac{\pi}{4} \right)\) in polar coordinates.

  2. Again using equations (9.6), but this time with \(x=-3\) and \(y=4\) gives \(r^2=(-3)^2+4^2=25\) or \(r=5\) and \(\tan\theta = -\frac{4}{3}\text{.}\) Since the point \((-3,4)\) lies in quadrant II, we compute \(\theta = \pi+\arctan(-4/3) \approx 2.21\) radians.

  3. Here \(r^2=0^2+3^2 = 9\) so that \(r=3\text{.}\) From equations (9.6) v we also have \(\tan\theta = \frac{3}{0}\text{.}\) Direct application of the second equation leads to division by zero! Graphing the point \((0,3)\) on the rectangular coordinate system reveals that the point is located on the positive \(y\)-axis. The angle between the positive \(x\)-axis and the positive \(y\)-axis is \(\frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\) Therefore this point can be represented as \(\left(3,\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\) in polar coordinates.

  4. Using equations (9.6), with \(x=5\sqrt{3}\) and \(y=-5\) gives \(r^2=(5\sqrt{3})^2+(-5)^2=100\) or \(r=10\) and \(\tan\theta = \frac{-5}{5\sqrt{3}} = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\) or \(\theta = -\frac{\pi}{6}\text{.}\) The point \((5\sqrt{3},-5)\) can thus be represented as \(\left( 10, -\frac{\pi}{6} \right)\) in polar coordinates.

Example 9.3.4. Converting from polar to rectangular coordinates.

Convert each of the following from polar to rectangular coordinates.

  1. \(\displaystyle (3,\pi/3)\)

  2. \(\displaystyle (2,3\pi/2)\)

  3. \(\displaystyle (6,-5\pi/6)\)

Solution.

  1. Using equations (9.7) with \(r=3\) and \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{3}\) gives \(x=3\cos (\pi/3) = \frac{3}{2}\) and \(y=3\sin (\pi/3) = \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}\text{.}\) Therefore this point can be represented as \(\left( \frac{3}{2}, \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2} \right)\) in rectangular coordinates.

  2. Using equations (9.7) with \(r=2\) and \(\theta = \frac{3\pi}{2}\) gives \(x=2\cos (3\pi/2) = 0\) and \(y=2\sin (3\pi/2) = -2\text{.}\) Therefore this point can be represented as \(0, -2 \right)\) in rectangular coordinates.

  3. Using equations (9.7) with \(r=6\) and \(\theta = -\frac{5\pi}{6}\) gives \(x=6\cos (-5\pi/6) = -3\sqrt{3}\) and \(y=6\sin (-5\pi/6) = -3\text{.}\) Therefore this point can be represented as \(\left( -3\sqrt{3}, -3 \right)\) in rectangular coordinates.

The polar representation of a point is not unique. For example, the polar coordinates \(\left( 2, \frac{\pi}{3} \right)\) and \(\left( 2, \frac{7\pi}{3}\right)\) both represent the point \((1,\sqrt{3})\) in the rectangular system. Also, the value of \(r\) can be negative. Therefore, the point with polar coordinates \(\left(-2,\frac{4\pi}{3}\right)\) also represents the point \((1,\sqrt{3})\) in the rectangular system. Every point in the plane has an infinite number of representations in polar coordinates. However, each point in the plane has only one representation in the rectangular coordinate system.

Subsection 9.3.2 Polar Curves

Now that we know how to plot points in the polar coordinate system, we can discuss how to plot curves. In the rectangular coordinate system, we can graph a function \(y=f(x)\) and create a curve in the Cartesian plane. In a similar fashion, we can graph a curve that is generated by a function \(r=f(θ)\text{.}\)

The general idea behind graphing a function in polar coordinates is the same as graphing a function in rectangular coordinates. Start with a list of values for the independent variable (\(\theta\) in this case) and calculate the corresponding values of the dependent variable \(r\text{.}\) This process generates a list of ordered pairs, which can be plotted in the polar coordinate system. Finally, connect the points, and take advantage of any patterns that may appear. The function may be periodic, for example, which indicates that only a limited number of values for the independent variable are needed.

Example 9.3.6. Graphing a function in polar coordinates.

Graph the curve defined by the function \(r=4\sin\theta\text{.}\) Identify the curve and rewrite the equation in rectangular coordinates.

Solution.

Because the function is a multiple of a sine function, it is periodic with period \(2\pi\text{,}\) so use values for \(\theta\) between \(0\) and \(2\pi\) in creating a table (see Table 9.3.7) of ordered pairs to be plotted (see Figure 9.3.8).

This is the graph of a circle. The equation \(r=4\sin\theta\) can be converted into rectangular coordinates by first multiplying both sides by \(r\text{.}\) This gives the equation \(r^2=4r\sin\theta\text{.}\) Next use the facts that \(r^2=x^2+y^2\)and \(y=r\sin\theta\text{.}\) This gives \(x^2+y^2=4y\text{.}\) To put this equation into standard form, subtract \(4y\) from both sides of the equation and complete the square:

\begin{align*} x^2+y^2 - 4y = \mathstrut \amp 0\\ x^2 + (y^2-4y) = \mathstrut \amp 0\\ x^2+(y^2-4y+4) = \mathstrut \amp 0+4\\ x^2 + (y-2)^2 = \mathstrut \amp 4. \end{align*}

This is the equation of a circle with radius \(2\) and center \((0,2)\) in the rectangular coordinate system.

The graph in Example 9.3.6 was that of a circle. The equation of the circle can be transformed into rectangular coordinates using the coordinate transformation formulas in equations (9.7).

Example 9.3.9. Transforming polar equations to rectangle coordinates.

Rewrite each of the following equations in rectangular coordinates and identify the graph.

  1. \(\displaystyle \theta = \frac{\pi}{3}\)

  2. \(\displaystyle r=3\)

  3. \(\displaystyle r=6\cos\theta - 8\sin\theta\)

Solution.

  1. Take the tangent of both sides. This gives \(\tan\theta = \tan(\pi/3) = \sqrt{3}\text{.}\) Since \(\tan\theta = \frac{y}{x}\) we can replace the left-hand side of this equation by \(\frac{y}{x}\text{.}\) This gives \(\frac{y}{x}=\sqrt{3}\text{,}\) which can be rewritten as \(y=\sqrt{3}x\text{.}\)

  2. First, square both sides of the equation. This gives \(r^2=9\text{.}\) Next replace \(r^2\) with \(x^2+y^2\text{.}\) This gives the equation \(x^2+y^2=9\text{,}\) which is the equation of a circle centered at the origin with radius 3. (Note: when squaring both sides of an equation it is possible to introduce new points unintentionally. This should always be taken into consideration. However, in this case we do not introduce new points. For example, \(\left(-3,\frac{\pi}{3}\right)\) is the same point as \(\left( 3,\frac{4\pi}{3}\right)\text{.}\))

  3. Multiply both sides of the equation by \(r\text{.}\) This leads to \(r^2=6r\cos\theta - 8r\sin\theta\text{.}\) Next use the formulas

    \begin{equation*} r^2=x^2+y^2, \ x=r\cos\theta, \ y=r\sin\theta\text{.} \end{equation*}

    This gives

    \begin{align*} r^2 = \mathstrut \amp 6(r\cos\theta) - 8(r\sin\theta)\\ x^2+y^2 = \mathstrut \amp 6x-8y. \end{align*}

    To put this equation into standard form, first move the variables from the right-hand side of the equation to the left-hand side, then complete the square.

    \begin{align*} x^2+y^2 = \mathstrut \amp 6x-8y\\ x^2-6x+y^2+8y = \mathstrut \amp 0\\ (x^2-6x)+(y^2+8y) = \mathstrut \amp 0\\ (x^2-6x+9)+(y^2+8y+16) = \mathstrut \amp 9+16\\ (x-3)^2+(y+4)^2 = \mathstrut \amp 25. \end{align*}

    This is the equation of a circle with center at \((3,-4)\) and radius \(5\text{.}\) Notice that the circle passes through the origin since the center is \(5\) units away.

Technology is helpful in plotting polar curves. For example, WolframAlpha can be utilized to produce the graph of \(r=3\sin (3\theta)\) using syntax

plot(r=3*sin(3*theta),theta=0..2*pi)

The resulting graph is shown in Figure 9.3.11. One should always be careful in utilizing technology however. It turns out that the plot of \(r=3\sin (3\theta)\) is the same if we let the parameter \(\theta\) range over any interval of length at least \(2\pi\text{.}\) For example,

plot(r=3*sin(3*theta),theta=0..100)

also produces the output shown in Figure 9.3.11. So Figure 9.3.11 is indeed the plot of the curve \(r=3\sin (3\theta)\text{.}\)

Now, what happens if we try to plot the polar equation \(r=3\sin (\pi \theta)\) using xWolframAlpha? Plotting the polar equations over the intervals \(0 \leq \theta \leq 3\text{,}\) \(0 \leq \theta \leq 100\text{,}\) and \(0 \leq \theta \leq 1000\) respectively will produce the output shown in Figure 9.3.12. Since the curve defined by the graph of \(r=3\sin (\pi \theta)\) never closes, the curves depicted in Figure 9.3.12 are only partial depictions. In fact, this is an example of a space-filling curve. A space-filling curve is one that occupies a two-dimensional subset of the real plane. In this case the curve occupies the circle of radius \(3\) centered at the origin.

Subsection 9.3.3 Symmetry in Polar Coordinates

Using rectangular coordinates, there are three types of symmetry - \(y\)-axis, \(x\)-axis, and origin.

  • A graph has symmetry with respect to the \(y\)-axis if, whenever \((x,y)\) is on the graph, so is the point \((-x,y)\text{.}\)

  • A graph has symmetry with respect to the \(x\)-axis if, whenever \((x,y)\) is on the graph, so is the point \((x,-y)\text{.}\)

  • A graph has symmetry with respect to the origin if, whenever \((x,y)\) is on the graph, so is the point \((-x,-y)\text{.}\)

Similarly, we describe three types of symmetry in polar coordinates.

  • Symmetry with respect to the polar axis: For every point \((r,\theta)\) on the graph, there is also a point reflected directly across the horizontal (polar) axis.

  • Symmetry with respect to the pole: For every point \((r,\theta)\) on the graph, there is also a point on the graph that is reflected through the pole as well.

  • Symmetry with respect to the vertical line \(\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}\text{:}\) For every point \((r,\theta)\) on the graph, there is also a point on the graph reflected directly across the vertical axis.

Symmetry tests in polar coordinates.

  • A graph has symmetry with respect to the polar axis if, whenever \((r,\theta)\) is on the graph, so is the point \((r,-\theta)\text{.}\)

  • A graph has symmetry with respect to the vertical axis if, whenever \((r,\theta)\) is on the graph, so is the point \((-r,-\theta)\text{.}\)

  • A graph has symmetry with respect to the pole if, whenever \((r,\theta)\) is on the graph, so is the point \((r,\pi+\theta)\text{.}\)

Subsection 9.3.4 Summary

  • The polar representation of a point \(P\) is the ordered pair \((r,\theta)\) where \(r\) is the distance from the origin to \(P\) and \(\theta\) is the angle the ray through the origin and \(P\) makes with the positive \(x\)-axis.

  • The polar coordinates \(r\) and \(\theta\) of a point \((x,y)\) in rectangular coordinates satisfy

    \begin{equation*} r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2} \ \ \ \ \ \text{ and } \ \ \ \ \ \tan(\theta) = \frac{y}{x}; \end{equation*}

    the rectangular coordinates \(x\) and \(y\) of a point \((r,\theta)\) in polar coordinates satisfy

    \begin{equation*} x = r\cos(\theta) \ \ \ \ \ \text{ and } \ \ \ \ \ y = r\sin(\theta). \end{equation*}

  • Just as one can convert between rectangular coordinates and polar coordinates, equations in these coordinate systems can also be converted.

  • Testing for symmetry in polar equations is similar to testing for symmetry in rectangular coordinates.
